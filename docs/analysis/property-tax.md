# Property Tax: The Most Important Tax Nobody Talks About

!!! info "Research in Progress"
    This analysis is currently under development. The data pipeline is operational
    and source collection is underway. Expected publication: Q3 2026.

## What This Analysis Will Cover

Property taxes generate 27.4% of combined state and local tax revenue and 70.2% of local government own-source revenue. They are administered by approximately 13,000 separate taxing jurisdictions with enormous variation — from effective rates above 3% in some New Jersey municipalities to below 0.3% in parts of Hawaii. Despite this importance, property tax receives far less analytical attention than income or sales taxes.

This analysis will use the Lincoln Institute of Land Policy's 50-State Property Tax Comparison Study as a foundation to map effective property tax rates across jurisdictions, examine assessment practices and their equity implications, and evaluate the growing use of property tax limitations (caps, freezes, and circuit breakers). We will also explore the relationship between property tax reliance and service quality, particularly in K-12 education funding.

The assessment process itself — how properties are valued, how often, and how uniformly — is a critical but underexamined source of inequity. Research consistently shows that lower-value properties tend to be assessed at higher ratios relative to market value, creating a regressive tilt within a nominally uniform tax.

## Data Sources

- Lincoln Institute of Land Policy 50-State Property Tax Comparison Study
- Census Bureau property tax collections data
- International Association of Assessing Officers (IAAO) assessment standards
- State equalization studies and ratio reports

## Preliminary Findings

The Lincoln Institute property tax data pipeline is currently under development. Once operational, it will provide standardized effective tax rate comparisons across jurisdictions, enabling systematic analysis of rate variation, assessment quality, and the impact of property tax limitations.
