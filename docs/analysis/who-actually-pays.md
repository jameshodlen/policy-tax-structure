# Who Actually Pays? The Methodology Wars

> The Tax Foundation says Florida has the 4th best tax system. ITEP says Florida has the most regressive. They're both right — and understanding why is the key to evaluating any claim about state tax policy.

## The Core Disagreement

Two organizations dominate the analysis of state tax structures in the United States:

- **The Tax Foundation** (founded 1937, center-right) asks: *Does this tax structure promote economic growth?*
- **ITEP** (Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy, founded 1980, center-left) asks: *Who bears the burden of this tax structure?*

These are different questions. They produce different metrics, different rankings, and different policy conclusions. Most public discussion cites one framework without acknowledging the other exists. This analysis presents both.

## The Data: 50-State Effective Tax Rates

ITEP's Who Pays? report calculates effective state and local tax rates by income quintile. Their headline finding:

!!! quote "ITEP, Who Pays? 6th Edition"
    "Virtually every state's tax system is fundamentally unfair, taking a much greater share of income from low- and middle-income families than from wealthy families. The absence of a graduated personal income tax and the low tax rate on capital gains income are the main contributors to this tax inequality."

## Why They Disagree: Five Methodological Differences

### 1. Retiree Inclusion

- **Tax Foundation**: Includes retirees in all analyses
- **ITEP**: Excludes retirees from distributional analysis

*Impact*: Excluding retirees makes tax systems appear slightly more regressive, because retirees with low current income (but potentially significant assets) are removed from lower quintiles.

### 2. Corporate Tax Incidence

- **Tax Foundation**: Allocates corporate tax burden partly to workers and consumers
- **ITEP**: Excludes corporate taxes from individual distributional analysis

*Impact*: Including corporate tax incidence makes systems appear less regressive, as corporate taxes fall partly on higher-income capital owners.

### 3. Income Measurement

- **Tax Foundation**: Advocates for lifetime income perspective
- **ITEP**: Uses snapshot (annual) income

*Impact*: Lifetime income approaches reduce measured regressivity. Snapshot approaches increase it.

### 4. What Constitutes "Good" Policy

- **Tax Foundation**: Neutrality, simplicity, broad bases, low rates
- **ITEP**: Distributional equity, adequate revenue, fair burden sharing

*Impact*: These are fundamentally different values, not different readings of the same data.

### 5. Tax Expenditure Treatment

- **Tax Foundation**: Evaluates the code as written, including exemptions as part of the structure
- **ITEP**: Identifies tax expenditures as policy choices that disproportionately benefit higher-income taxpayers

For a detailed comparison, see [Tax Foundation vs ITEP](../methodology/tax-foundation-vs-itep.md).

## What the Data Cannot Tell Us

1. **Whether regressivity is "bad"** — That is a values judgment. A consumption tax advocate would argue that taxing consumption rather than income is economically efficient regardless of distributional effects. An equity advocate would argue that asking more of those who have less is unjust regardless of efficiency.

2. **Whether competitive tax structures cause growth** — States with low taxes may grow faster because of their tax structures, or because of other factors (climate, natural resources, industry mix) that also correlate with low-tax policies.

3. **Whether progressive taxes cause prosperity** — Minnesota's high taxes coexist with high incomes, but the causal relationship (if any) runs through decades of compounding policy choices, not a single tax rate.

4. **What happens next** — The flat tax revolution is transforming state tax structures in real time. Whether states that cut income taxes will see revenue shortfalls, spending cuts, or growth-driven revenue replacement is an empirical question that will take years to answer.

## Using This Analysis

This page is not designed to tell you which framework is "right." It is designed to equip you to evaluate claims about state tax policy by understanding:

- What data the claim is based on
- What assumptions underlie the analysis
- What the same data looks like through a different analytical lens
- What the data genuinely cannot answer

When a headline says "State X has the best tax system" or "State X has the most unfair taxes," you now know to ask: **By whose framework? Using what assumptions? And what does the other framework show?**
