# Emerging Tax Bases: Digital, Sports Betting, Cannabis, EVs

!!! info "Research in Progress"
    This analysis is currently under development. The data pipeline is operational
    and source collection is underway. Expected publication: Q4 2026.

## What This Analysis Will Cover

As traditional tax bases erode (remote sales, declining fuel consumption, digital services), states are tapping new revenue sources. Maryland's digital advertising tax faces constitutional challenges. Sports betting tax rates range from 6.75% (Nevada) to 51% (New York). Cannabis taxation varies from simple sales tax to complex THC-content-based structures. And 33+ states have enacted EV registration fees to replace lost gas tax revenue.

This analysis will examine each of these emerging tax bases in turn: the revenue generated so far, the policy design choices states have made, the legal and administrative challenges that have arisen, and the long-term revenue potential. We will compare rate structures, assess revenue stability, and evaluate whether these new bases are adequate substitutes for the traditional revenue streams they are partially replacing.

A recurring theme across all four areas is the tension between revenue generation and regulatory goals. Sports betting and cannabis taxes must balance revenue maximization against the risk of driving activity to illegal markets. Digital advertising and EV fees must balance revenue needs against concerns about economic competitiveness and regressivity.

## Data Sources

- State revenue department collections and reporting
- Tax Foundation rate tracking and comparative analyses
- Legislative tracking databases (NCSL, MultiState)

## Preliminary Findings

Sports betting is now legal in 38 states, with tax rates spanning an enormous range — from 6.75% in Nevada to 51% in New York. This rate spread offers a natural experiment in how tax rates affect market structure, operator viability, and the migration of betting activity between legal and illegal channels.
