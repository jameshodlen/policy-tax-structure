# Tax Foundation vs ITEP: Two Frameworks, Two Stories

This page is the platform's thesis statement. The Tax Foundation and ITEP (Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy) are the two most influential organizations analyzing state tax structures. They are both rigorous. They both use real data. And they frequently reach opposing conclusions about the same state's tax system.

Understanding *why* they disagree is essential to evaluating any claim about state tax policy.

## The Organizations

| | Tax Foundation | ITEP |
|---|---|---|
| **Founded** | 1937 | 1980 |
| **Political lean** | Center-right | Center-left |
| **Core question** | "Does this tax structure promote economic growth?" | "Who bears the burden of this tax structure?" |
| **Flagship product** | State Tax Competitiveness Index | Who Pays? Report |
| **Updated** | Annually | Periodically (6th edition: 2018) |
| **Emphasis** | Broad bases, low rates, neutrality, simplicity | Distributional equity, effective rates by income |
| **Funding** | Mix of foundation grants and corporate support | Foundation grants |

## Where They Agree

Both organizations agree on several foundational points:

- State tax structures vary enormously and those differences have real consequences
- Data transparency and methodological rigor matter
- The interaction between state and federal tax systems is significant
- Tax policy involves trade-offs — there is no free lunch

## Where They Disagree

### 1. What Counts as "Good" Tax Policy

**Tax Foundation**: A good tax system is **neutral** — it doesn't distort economic decisions. It has a broad base (few exemptions), low rates, and treats all types of income similarly. The State Tax Competitiveness Index ranks states on five components: corporate taxes, individual income taxes, sales taxes, property taxes, and unemployment insurance taxes.

**ITEP**: A good tax system is **fair** — it distributes the tax burden equitably across income levels. The Who Pays? report calculates effective state and local tax rates by income quintile and evaluates whether a state's system is regressive (lower-income pay higher effective rates) or progressive.

!!! note "Different questions, different answers"
    A state can rank well on the Competitiveness Index (broad base, low rates) and poorly on Who Pays? (regressive distributional impact) simultaneously. This is not a contradiction — it reflects the different questions each framework asks.

### 2. Who Is Included in the Analysis

**Tax Foundation**: Includes all taxpayers, including retirees. This matters because retirees often have low current income but significant assets and prior lifetime earnings.

**ITEP**: Excludes retirees from the distributional analysis. ITEP argues that including retirees distorts the picture of how the tax system treats working-age households, since retirees' low current income doesn't reflect their economic resources.

*Impact*: Excluding retirees tends to make tax systems look slightly more regressive, because retirees in lower income quintiles are replaced by working-age households with genuinely low earnings.

### 3. How Corporate Taxes Are Allocated

**Tax Foundation**: Includes the economic incidence of corporate income taxes and business property taxes in its analysis of who bears the tax burden. Following public finance literature, it allocates some corporate tax burden to workers (through lower wages) and consumers (through higher prices).

**ITEP**: Excludes corporate income tax and business property tax from its distributional analysis of individual tax burdens. ITEP focuses on taxes directly levied on individuals.

*Impact*: Including corporate tax incidence tends to make tax systems look less regressive, because corporate taxes are assumed to fall partly on higher-income capital owners.

### 4. Income Measurement

**Tax Foundation**: Argues for a **lifetime income** perspective. A 25-year-old medical resident earning $60,000 is not in the same economic position as a 55-year-old earning $60,000 with no prospect of income growth. Snapshot income overstates regressivity by treating temporarily low-income earners as permanently poor.

**ITEP**: Uses **snapshot (annual) income**. ITEP argues that people pay taxes on current income, not future income, and that tax policy should be evaluated on its current-year impact. Most people in the bottom quintile are not temporarily there.

*Impact*: Lifetime income approaches tend to make tax systems look less regressive. Snapshot approaches tend to make them look more regressive.

### 5. What "Regressive" Means

**Tax Foundation**: Questions whether "regressive" is the right frame for evaluating state tax systems. A flat sales tax is regressive as a share of income, but it's proportional to consumption. If the goal is to fund public services efficiently with minimal economic distortion, a broad-based sales tax may be preferable even if it's regressive in distributional terms.

**ITEP**: Treats regressivity as a core metric. ITEP's Tax Inequality Index specifically measures how much a state's tax system increases or decreases income inequality. Their flagship finding: the lowest-income 20% of Americans pay an average 11.4% effective state and local tax rate nationally, compared to 7.2% for the top 1%.

## The Same State, Two Stories

Consider a state like **Florida** (no income tax, 6% sales tax, heavy reliance on tourism taxes and property taxes):

=== "Tax Foundation View"

    Florida ranks **4th** on the State Tax Competitiveness Index (2024). Its lack of an individual income tax is a major competitive advantage. The broad-based sales tax is relatively neutral. The structure encourages business formation and interstate migration of high earners. Florida's tax structure promotes economic growth.

=== "ITEP View"

    Florida has the **most regressive** tax system in the country (Who Pays?, 6th edition). The lowest-income 20% of Floridians pay a 12.7% effective state and local tax rate, while the top 1% pay just 2.3%. The absence of an income tax means Florida cannot use its tax code to offset the regressivity of sales and property taxes.

=== "What the Data Shows"

    Both characterizations are factually accurate. Florida's tax structure *is* competitively advantageous by Tax Foundation metrics. It *is* distributionally regressive by ITEP metrics. The question is which dimension matters more — and that is a values question, not a data question.

## Why This Matters

Most public discussion of state tax policy cites one framework or the other without acknowledging the other exists. Headlines like "State X has the best tax climate" (Tax Foundation) or "State X has the most unfair tax system" (ITEP) are both defensible — and both incomplete.

This platform presents both frameworks for every state. We show the data, we show the assumptions behind each framework's analysis, and we show where the data is genuinely ambiguous. The goal is not to split the difference or find a "moderate" position. The goal is transparency about what the evidence can and cannot tell us.
